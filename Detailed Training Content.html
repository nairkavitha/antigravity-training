01

Segment 1 — Welcome & The Shift in Mindset

Our workshop centers on a key change in how you approach AI tools:

“For the next five days, you are not learning to code. You are learning to direct. The agent is your Expert Contractor — highly capable, extremely fast, but operating with zero institutional knowledge and zero judgment about consequences. It will do exactly what you asked, not what you meant. A human team member would flag when something seems off. The agent won't — it will produce a clean, confident output and wait for your approval. That's why your sign-off is not a formality. It's the only safeguard in the chain.”

What to Expect: You will not write code during this workshop. The sandbox is a safe-to-fail practicing space. By Day 4, you will run an annual pay review end-to-end yourself.

02

Segment 2 — Navigating the Workspace

To navigate our workspace, we use the Integrated Command Desk (IDE). Just as a physical office desk puts your paperwork, folders, and instruments in one place, the Command Desk integrates your file editor, AI agent chat, browser, and terminal into a single window. In technical manuals, developers call this an "IDE" (Integrated Development Environment), but we call it our Command Desk because it is where all our work, tools, and sandbox live.

03

Segment 3 — Agents, LLMs & Hallucination

  • Chatbot vs. Agent: A chatbot simply responds to text prompts (like an advisor on the phone). An AI Agent acts—it plans, navigates folders, runs tools, and creates documents (like a team member who does the work).
  • LLM: the engine underneath — a prediction machine trained on language. It is powerful but not a database; it predicts plausible text. When the agent reads your files, it's not guessing — it's pulling the actual data. Where it predicts is in interpreting that data, making connections, and writing the analysis. The numbers it reads are real. The conclusions it draws need checking.
  • Hallucination: because it predicts rather than looks up, it can state a wrong figure confidently. This is THE reason we keep a human in the loop and always check artifacts against source files.
⚠️
Key Concept: Confidence is not Correctness. Because LLMs predict plausible text rather than searching a static database, they can state wrong facts with absolute confidence (known as "hallucination"). This is why human review (Human-in-the-Loop) is the ultimate safeguard.

Q1
What is the difference between a chatbot and an agent?
A chatbot is like an advisor on the phone (it simply answers text questions). An AI Agent acts like a team member doing the work—it plans, reads and writes files, runs commands, and creates documents to achieve a goal.
Q2
Why must we always review and check the agent's work?
Because LLM agents are prediction engines, they can hallucinate and state incorrect facts or figures with absolute confidence. Checking calculations against source data ensures corporate policy compliance.

Day 1 — Delegating Goals vs. Giving Orders

Learning outcomes: Learners write an outcome-based brief (not a step list), read an implementation plan, give one round of feedback, and consciously approve.

01

Segment 1 — Mindset Shift: Goals vs. Commands

Compare how the agent interprets micro-managing commands versus outcome-driven goal briefs:

BAD (micro-managing):
Open employees.csv. Go to row 3. Create a folder. Name it Rohan.
Now make three subfolders...

GOOD (delegating):
Set up an onboarding folder structure for our new hire Rohan Verma,
with places for his contracts, benefits, and compliance documents.
Show me your plan before creating anything.

The lesson: describe the outcome and the constraints, then let the contractor propose the how. Over-specifying wastes the agent's intelligence.

02

Segment 2 — The Plan ➔ Act ➔ Verify Loop

We coordinate with the agent using a standard Plan ➔ Act ➔ Verify rhythm. The agent proposes a plan, you iterate with feedback, and you approve the plan before execution begins.

Good start, but put compliance documents BEFORE benefits, and add a

03

Segment 3 — Hands-on Exercise: Onboarding Rohan Verma

  1. Brief the agent using the outcome-based template. Prompt: "Set up an onboarding structure... Show me your plan first."
  2. Open and read the newly created implementation_plan.md. Request exactly one modification (e.g. folder order or letter template subfolder).
  3. Approve the revised plan and watch the agent build the folders under exercises/ in real-time.
⚠️
Caution: Avoid Auto-Approvals. The implementation plan approval step is your primary governance control. If you approve a plan without reading it, you are signing off on unknown actions.
04

Debrief (10 min)

💡 Interactive Knowledge Check

Q1
What changed when you stopped giving step-by-step commands and started giving outcome-based goals?
You transitioned from coding/micromanaging to directing. Outcome-based goals allow the agent to use its reasoning capability to find the best path, saving time and keeping you focused on guardrails.
Q2
When is the right moment to catch a mistake: before or after the agent executes the changes?
Before—at the planning stage. Relentlessly auditing the implementation_plan.md lets you correct errors before files are written or directories created.

Day 2 — Navigating the Workspace & Tools

Learning outcomes: Learners understand tool calls and permissions, run a real data analysis on the sandbox, and read a compa-ratio result. Security awareness is introduced.

01

Segment 1 — Agent Tools & Permissions

An agent interacts with files and directories through Tool Calls (e.g. reading a file or executing a query). MCP (Model Context Protocol) functions as a secure connector to these resources. As the director, you must review and approve each tool call, akin to granting a contractor keys to a specific filing cabinet drawer one at a time.

🔒
Data Security Rule: Compensation and employee PII must remain inside the sandboxed folder. Never paste employee data, CSV lists, or compensation formulas into external, public chatbots.
02

Segment 2 — Understanding the Compa-Ratio

Open the dataset and review the formula used to analyze employee positioning in the market:

Compa-Ratio = Current Salary / Market Midpoint
( < 1.0 = paid below market ;  ~1.0 = on market ;  > 1.0 = above )

Show market_rates.csv bands (Grade 1–4) and that employees.csv has a grade + rating + salary per person.

03

Segment 3 — Hands-on Exercise: Identifying Compensation Discrepancies

Analyse our current compensation in employees.csv against the market
bands in market_rates.csv. Flag anyone whose salary sits below the
market minimum for their grade, and show me the list with their
compa-ratio. Don't change any files — just report.
👉 Verification Task: Once the agent returns the flagged list, select one employee and perform a manual math audit (Compa-Ratio = Salary / Market Midpoint) to confirm that the agent's calculation is correct.
04

Debrief (10 min)

💡 Interactive Knowledge Check

Q1
What should you check before granting tool call permissions to the agent?
Verify that the targeted file path is correct, the action conforms to the approved plan, and the command does not leak sensitive information outside the workspace boundaries.
Q2
Where could PII have leaked, and what corporate governance guideline protects it?
PII could leak if the agent makes a network request or if you copy sensitive tables into a public AI tool. The sandboxing rule ensures salary data never leaves the HR_Mission_Control environment.

Day 3 — Agentic Orchestration & Subagents

Learning outcomes: Learners coordinate two specialist subagents, read a Thought Process as an audit trail, and merge findings into one report.

01

Segment 1 — Multi-Agent Orchestration

Complex tasks can be split across specialized **subagents** operating in parallel (e.g. an Equity Auditor and a Market Analyst). You will monitor their coordination through the **Thought Process** panel, which acts as a live audit trail allowing you to inspect their logic before they request permissions.

02

Segment 2 — Tracing Subagent Activities

Review how a subagent plans and reads files. Observe the Thought Process logs to verify its strategy step-by-step.

03

Segment 3 — Hands-on Exercise: Deploying Specialized Subagents

Deploy two specialist subagents to analyse employees.csv:
  1) a "Gender Pay Equity Auditor" — compare pay of men vs women
     within the same grade and role, and surface any gaps.
  2) a "Market Positioning Analyst" — compare everyone to their
     grade market midpoint and group them as below / on / above market.
Then merge both findings into one summary report under exercises/.
💡 Reconciling Outputs: If the subagents return conflicting figures in their final files, open the Thought Process history of both agents to trace where the formula or data source diverged.
04

Debrief (10 min)

💡 Interactive Knowledge Check

Q1
What does reading the agent's Thought Process let you check that a final report alone won't show?
It reveals the intermediate logic steps, how the formulas were translated, and any incorrect assumptions the agent made before compiling the final summary.
Q2
When is multi-agent orchestration worth deploying, and when is it overkill?
It is worth deploying for multi-dimensional tasks that can run in parallel (like equity checks vs. market positioning). It is overkill for simple, linear file edits.

Day 4 — Building the Pay Review Tool (Capstone)

Learning outcomes: Learners run a full, policy-compliant FY 2026-27 pay review end-to-end, produce the results CSV and draft letters, and exercise all three HITL checkpoints. This is the graduation moment.

01

Segment 1 — Annual Compensation Guidelines

Before launching the review tool, align on the core compliance guardrails laid out in the guidelines:

  1. Apply the performance rating grid (Outstanding 12–15%, … Needs Improvement 0%).
  1. Market correction first: if below market_min and rating ≥ Meets, lift up to 10% (or to market_min, whichever is lower) BEFORE the performance increase.
  1. Over-market cap: if above market_max, cap total increase at 3%.
  1. Budget cap: all increases together must stay within 8% of total payroll.
02

Segment 2 — Capstone Project: Running the Pay Review

Run the FY 2026-27 pay review for everyone in employees.csv.
Follow compensation_guidelines.md exactly — apply market corrections,
the performance grid, the over-market cap, and keep the total within
the 8% budget cap. Show me your plan and the calculations for sign-off
BEFORE writing anything. Then generate pay_review_results.csv and draft
individual letters under letters/ using the template.
  • HITL Checkpoint 1 (Plan Stage): Review computed compa-ratios and proposed increase lists before letting the agent generate the results.
  • HITL Checkpoint 2 (Budget Stage): Flag any payroll adjustments exceeding the 8% budget limit or performance ratings constraints.
  • HITL Checkpoint 3 (Audit Stage): Manually verify that drafted letters in letters/ match the calculated compensation adjustments.
💡 Troubleshooting Recovery: If the agent reports it cannot satisfy both the budget limit and performance bands, prompt: "Pause and report which specific constraint is blocking you." Use this feedback to override or balance guidelines.
03

Segment 3 — Saving Workbook Progress

Verify your generated CSV and drafted compensation letters. Then, run a Git Commit to establish a permanent save point for your capstone project deliverables.

04

Program Wrap-up & Reflection

💡 Interactive Knowledge Check

Q1
Where did a compensation policy guardrail stop the agent from doing the wrong thing in the capstone?
The budget limit cap (8% total payroll) and the performance-band rating grid prevented the agent from granting out-of-policy raises to employees.
Q2
How does being an 'HR Director' of an AI agent differ from traditional manual operations?
Instead of performing manual data entries and compiling spreadsheets, you act as a systems auditor. You direct the outcomes, define policy guardrails, and audit output verification.
