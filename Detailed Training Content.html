Day 0 — Welcome to the Command Desk

Learning outcomes: By the end, every learner has Antigravity installed and open, can explain in plain words what an AI Agent is (vs a chatbot), recognises the five context files, and has run one safe agent task.

Materials: Installer link, authorised Google account, the HR_Mission_Control folder pre-placed on each desktop, glossary.html, a projector for the live demo.

Segment 1 — Welcome & the big reframe (10 min)

Open with the one idea the whole course hangs off:

“For the next five days, you are not learning to code. You are learning to direct. The agent is your Expert Contractor — highly capable, extremely fast, but operating with zero institutional knowledge and zero judgment about consequences. It will do exactly what you asked, not what you meant. A human team member would flag when something seems off. The agent won't — it will produce a clean, confident output and wait for your approval. That's why your sign-off is not a formality. It's the only safeguard in the chain.”

Set expectations: nobody will write code; the sandbox is safe-to-fail; mistakes are how we learn. Name the destination — by Day 4 they will run a full pay review themselves.

Segment 2 — What is an IDE? (10 min)

Teach the Integrated Command Desk analogy: instead of Word for writing, a browser for testing, and Explorer for files in three separate windows, an IDE puts the desk, filing cabinet and testing room in one place. Explain that while software developers call this an "IDE" (Integrated Development Environment), we call it our Command Desk because it is where all our work, tools, and sandbox live. Point at the screen and name the panels as you go.

Segment 3 — Agents, LLMs & hallucination (20 min)

  • Chatbot vs. Agent: a chatbot talks back; an agent acts — it plans, opens files, runs tools, produces artifacts. Use the “advisor on the phone vs. team member who does the work” contrast.
  • LLM: the engine underneath — a prediction machine trained on language. It is powerful but not a database; it predicts plausible text. When the agent reads your files, it's not guessing — it's pulling the actual data. Where it predicts is in interpreting that data, making connections, and writing the analysis. The numbers it reads are real. The conclusions it draws need checking.
  • Hallucination: because it predicts rather than looks up, it can state a wrong figure confidently. This is THE reason we keep a human in the loop and always check artifacts against source files.

Segment 4 — The five context files (15 min)

Walk the “paperwork” the agent lives by. Keep it conceptual — what each is FOR:

  • README.md — the welcome sign: explains the project structure.
  • context.md — the standing brief: the rules and policies the agent must always follow.
  • implementation_plan.md — the project proposal: the agent's plan, drafted for your sign-off.
  • task.md — the whiteboard: live progress, ticked off as work happens.
  • handover.md — the shift report: the summary when work passes on.

Segment 5 — Install Antigravity 2.0 (45 min, follow-along)

  1. Download & run the installer for your OS (Windows/macOS).
  1. Authenticate with your authorised Google account.
  1. Click Open Workspace and select the pre-placed HR_Mission_Control folder. Emphasise: We've configured the sandbox so the agent works within this folder only. Don't change the workspace path — that boundary is the foundation of your data security for this programme.

Segment 6 — Interface tour (30 min)

  • Manager Surface (the Control Room): spawn agents, watch several run in parallel, schedule background routines. [Participant Action: Find the "spawn agent" button and hover over it.]
  • Editor View (the Work Desk): the Prompt Bar (where you brief the agent), the integrated Browser & Terminal (watch it work live). [Participant Action: Click the Prompt Bar.]
  • Artifacts & Trust: Task Lists, screenshots/walkthroughs, and Google-Doc-style comments to steer mid-task. This is the most important feature for HR — it is how you audit. When the agent produces a document, this panel is where you compare the output against the source file before approving. [Participant Action: Open one artifact the facilitator has pre-placed and read the first line.]

Segment 7 — First prompt (15 min)

Run a 30-second silent setup verification prompt (everyone runs it, facilitator confirms the files are present):

List the files inside HR_Mission_Control/data/

Now delegate the actual first prompt to see a value-driven HR output and value proposition immediately:

We've just hired Rohan Verma as a new HR Business Partner. List the documents he'll need for onboarding, organised by category.

Then have them open glossary.html in the browser and study 5 flashcards of their choice.

Debrief (5 min)

  • Ask: “What is the difference between a chatbot and an agent?”
  • Ask: “Why do we always check the agent's work?” (Answer: hallucination.)
  • Ask: “If the agent gives you a salary analysis tomorrow that looks complete and well-formatted, what's the one thing you'd do before presenting it to your manager?” (Answer: "Check it against the source data" to verify accuracy.)

Day 1 — Delegating Goals vs. Giving Orders

Learning outcomes: Learners write an outcome-based brief (not a step list), read an implementation plan, give one round of feedback, and consciously approve.

Segment 1 — The mindset shift (15 min)

Put two prompts on screen side by side and discuss how the agent would respond to each:

BAD (micro-managing):
Open employees.csv. Go to row 3. Create a folder. Name it Rohan.
Now make three subfolders...

GOOD (delegating):
Set up an onboarding folder structure for our new hire Rohan Verma,
with places for his contracts, benefits, and compliance documents.
Show me your plan before creating anything.

The lesson: describe the outcome and the constraints, then let the contractor propose the how. Over-specifying wastes the agent's intelligence.

Segment 2 — Plan → Act → Verify, live (15 min)

Demo the rhythm: give a goal, let the agent produce implementation_plan.md, then deliberately reject it once with feedback so learners see iteration before they try it.

Good start, but put compliance documents BEFORE benefits, and add a
subfolder for his signed offer letter. Update the plan and show me again.

Segment 3 — Hands-on: onboard Rohan Verma (40 min)

  1. Brief the goal (outcome-based). Ask for a plan first.
  1. Read implementation_plan.md. Request exactly one change.
  1. Approve, and watch the agent build the folder tree under exercises/.

Debrief (10 min)

  • What changed when you stopped giving steps and started giving outcomes?
  • When is the right moment to catch a mistake — before or after the agent acts? (Before: at the plan.)

Day 2 — Navigating the Workspace & Tools

Learning outcomes: Learners understand tool calls and permissions, run a real data analysis on the sandbox, and read a compa-ratio result. Security awareness is introduced.

Segment 1 — Tools, permissions & MCP (20 min)

Teach the Tool Call as the agent reaching for an instrument (read file, search, run command). MCP is the temporary gate pass that lets the agent plug into a data source. You watch each tool call before granting it — like giving an intern the cabinet keys one drawer at a time.

Segment 2 — The HR data + compa-ratio (15 min)

Open the two data files and the policy doc together; explain the one formula they need:

Compa-Ratio = Current Salary / Market Midpoint
( < 1.0 = paid below market ;  ~1.0 = on market ;  > 1.0 = above )

Show market_rates.csv bands (Grade 1–4) and that employees.csv has a grade + rating + salary per person.

Segment 3 — Hands-on: flag the underpaid (35 min)

Analyse our current compensation in employees.csv against the market
bands in market_rates.csv. Flag anyone whose salary sits below the
market minimum for their grade, and show me the list with their
compa-ratio. Don't change any files — just report.
  • Expect: the agent reads both CSVs, computes compa-ratios, returns a flagged list.
  • Have learners audit one flagged person by hand to confirm the agent is right (hallucination check).

Debrief (10 min)

  • What did you check before approving the agent's tool calls?
  • Where could PII have leaked, and what stopped it?

Day 3 — Agentic Orchestration & Subagents

Learning outcomes: Learners coordinate two specialist subagents, read a Thought Process as an audit trail, and merge findings into one report.

Segment 1 — Why orchestrate (15 min)

Frame the HR Manager coordinating specialists. Teach when to split work (distinct, parallel angles) vs. keep it single-threaded (one linear task). Introduce the Thought Process as the live audit trail you read to catch drift early.

Segment 2 — Demo one subagent (15 min)

Spawn a single subagent and read its Thought Process aloud, narrating what it intends before it acts — so learners see transparency in action.

Segment 3 — Hands-on: two subagents (40 min)

Deploy two specialist subagents to analyse employees.csv:
  1) a "Gender Pay Equity Auditor" — compare pay of men vs women
     within the same grade and role, and surface any gaps.
  2) a "Market Positioning Analyst" — compare everyone to their
     grade market midpoint and group them as below / on / above market.
Then merge both findings into one summary report under exercises/.
  • Coach reconciling conflicting findings — what to do when two subagents disagree.

Debrief (10 min)

  • What did the Thought Process let you catch that a final answer alone wouldn't?
  • When is orchestration worth it, and when is it overkill?

Day 4 — Building the Pay Review Tool (Capstone)

Learning outcomes: Learners run a full, policy-compliant FY 2026-27 pay review end-to-end, produce the results CSV and draft letters, and exercise all three HITL checkpoints. This is the graduation moment.

Segment 1 — The guardrail chain (20 min)

Walk the full policy logic on screen, in order, so learners know what 'correct' looks like before the agent runs:

  1. Apply the performance rating grid (Outstanding 12–15%, … Needs Improvement 0%).
  1. Market correction first: if below market_min and rating ≥ Meets, lift up to 10% (or to market_min, whichever is lower) BEFORE the performance increase.
  1. Over-market cap: if above market_max, cap total increase at 3%.
  1. Budget cap: all increases together must stay within 8% of total payroll.

Segment 2 — Hands-on capstone (60 min)

Run the FY 2026-27 pay review for everyone in employees.csv.
Follow compensation_guidelines.md exactly — apply market corrections,
the performance grid, the over-market cap, and keep the total within
the 8% budget cap. Show me your plan and the calculations for sign-off
BEFORE writing anything. Then generate pay_review_results.csv and draft
individual letters under letters/ using the template.
  • HITL checkpoint 1: review compa-ratio calculations before increases are drafted.
  • HITL checkpoint 2: any increase breaching the budget cap or outside the grid is flagged for manual approval.
  • HITL checkpoint 3: audit the letters for accuracy and tone before they're 'final'.

Segment 3 — Wrap & save the work (15 min)

Have learners review their CSV + letters, then introduce Commit as the 'dated save point' to preserve the finished work. Close by mapping what they learned back to the five days.

Debrief & close (15 min)

  • Where did a guardrail stop the agent from doing the wrong thing?
  • How is being an 'HR Architect' different from how you work today?
“You didn't write a single line of code — but you ran a governed, auditable pay review. That's the shift: from doing the work to directing and verifying it.”
